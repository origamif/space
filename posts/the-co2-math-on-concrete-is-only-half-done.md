---
title: The CO2 Math on Concrete Is Only Half Done
date: 2026-05-14
tags: drop, environment, science, infrastructure
---

Concrete has reabsorbed nearly half the CO2 emitted to make it, and almost nobody counts that.

Every few months another headline rolls around: cement is responsible for 8% of global CO2 emissions. CBS ran the stat in a segment on Brimstone, a startup making low-carbon cement [1]. Wisconsin Public Radio repeated it [2]. The number is real enough. Cement production released about 4.1 billion tonnes of CO2 in 2023, putting it behind only China and the United States in total national-scale emissions [3]. What the headlines leave out is what happens next.

Cement carbonation is the chemical reversal of calcination. When calcium oxide in concrete reacts with atmospheric CO2 and moisture, it forms calcium carbonate again. The same CO2 released during manufacturing gets pulled back in over decades. Xi et al. found in a 2016 study in *Nature Geoscience* that between 1930 and 2013, cement materials had sequestered roughly 4.5 gigatonnes of carbon, offsetting 43% of process emissions [4]. That was the first major global estimate. The numbers have grown since.

Two recent datasets confirm the scale. Wu et al. (2024) in *Nature Scientific Data* calculated cumulative CO2 absorption of 23.89 Gt from 1930 through 2023, equivalent to 52.32% of cement process emissions [5]. Niu et al. (2025) in *Earth System Science Data* put the figure at 21.26 GtCO2 from 1928 to 2023, offsetting roughly 46% [3]. Different models, different data sources, same ballpark: about half the CO2 released during calcination comes back.

And that might be conservative. When concrete is demolished and crushed at end of life, the exposed surface area increases and carbonation accelerates. Saade et al. (2022) in *Environmental Research Letters* found that crushed concrete carbonation alone could offset over 9% of the cumulative warming effect of manufacturing [6].

But there is a catch. A 2024 paper in *Nature Communications* showed that the raw tonnage numbers overstate the climate benefit. Carbonation happens slowly over decades while the emissions happen instantly. When you account for timing, the actual warming reduction is about 60% smaller than the gross uptake figures suggest [7]. Half the CO2 comes back, but it comes back too late to matter as much.

So why does the "8% of global emissions" figure circulate without mentioning reabsorption? Because the accounting framework is incomplete. The 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories considered cement carbonation but declined to include it, citing uncertainty [8]. The 2019 Refinement also excluded it. In 2021, the IPCC's Emission Factor Database Editorial Board reviewed proposals to add carbonation uptake factors and declined, reasoning that lifetime totals did not fit the annual reporting format national inventories require [8]. There is now an active push to build a tiered accounting framework for integrating carbonation into IPCC guidelines [9]. As of today, almost no national inventory counts it.

None of this makes concrete clean. Even after carbonation offsets, net emissions are enormous. The Global Cement and Concrete Association's progress report, launched at COP30 in Belém in November 2025, claims a 25% reduction in CO2 intensity per tonne of cementitious material since 1990 [10]. But total production keeps climbing, and the chemistry sets a ceiling: Portland cement can theoretically reabsorb about 50% of process CO2 through carbonation. The other half, plus all fossil fuel emissions from kiln heating, stays in the atmosphere.

The issue is the ledger. Emissions get recorded the instant they leave the stack. Reabsorption plays out over 50 years and nobody writes it down. Getting the full carbon budget right does not make concrete green. It makes the numbers honest.
